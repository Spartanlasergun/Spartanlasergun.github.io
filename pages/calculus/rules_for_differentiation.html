Rules for Differentiation
The theoretical conception of the differential coefficient is the foundation of calculus, but there are broader rules for its application in the algebraic sense. These rules are no less theoretical, and they are meant to address the application of calculus to more complex problems invloving multiple variables and combinations of functions. This section will discuss how these things are done with respect to the first principles of calculus.
Notes on Rules
There are several elementary rules that serve as guidelines for applying calculus to more complex problems. These are known as the Product Rule, the Quotient Rule, and the Chain Rule. It's important to discuss the implications of these rules beforehand, because while they are a great starting point for a more complex understanding of calculus, they are just blunt instruments shaped by the first principles of calculus. Consequently, they serve little more than to develop one's intuition for algebraic manipulations of problems involving differentials. In this respect, I would advise giving greater consideration to their proofs rather than taking the identities wholesale.
