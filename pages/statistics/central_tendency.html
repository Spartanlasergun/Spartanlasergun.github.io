Mean, Mode and Median
The concept of central tendency has been studied for centuries in mathematics, and its origins can be traced back to ancient Greece. The Greek philosopher Aristotle wrote about the concept of the mean in his work "Politics", where he discussed how the mean can be used to find the balance between extremes.
Later on, in the 17th century, the French mathematician and philosopher Blaise Pascal developed the concept of the arithmetic mean, which is now one of the most commonly used measures of central tendency.
The concept of the mode, which is one of the measures of central tendency, has a long history and it is difficult to attribute its discovery to a single person. However, the use of the term "mode" is commonly attributed to the English mathematician Karl Pearson, who introduced the term in his influential book "The Grammar of Science" published in 1892.
The concept of the median, which is another measure of central tendency, was first introduced by the Persian mathematician Alhazen (also known as Ibn al-Haytham) in the 11th century, and later refined by other mathematicians such as the French mathematician Pierre-Simon Laplace in the 18th century.
