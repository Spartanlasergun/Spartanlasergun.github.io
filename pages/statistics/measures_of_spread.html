Measures of Spread
In statistics, measures of spread provide valuable information about the variability or spread of data points within a dataset. While measures of central tendency, such as the mean or median, offer insights into the typical or central value of a dataset, measures of spread help to understand how the data points are distributed around the central value. Though there are countless measurements that can be made with respect to the spread across a given dataset, over time several key fundamental ideas have taken precedence and are commonly used to describe the spread of data. These key ideas are discussed in this section.
Range & Interquartile Range
Range: The range is the simplest measure of spread, representing the difference between the largest and smallest values in a dataset. It provides an overall sense of the spread but can be sensitive to outliers.
Interquartile Range (IQR): The IQR represents the range between the first quartile (25th percentile) and the third quartile (75th percentile) of a dataset. It is robust to outliers and provides insights into the middle 50% of the data, making it useful for describing skewed distributions.
Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)
The Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD), measures the average absolute difference between each data point and the mean. The mean absolute deviation is a precise calculation whereas the standard deviation accounts for potential errors. This difference is critical to understanding why the MAD is used much less frequently in real life situations compared to the standard deviation. (This will be discussed further when we talk about the standard deviation.) Standard Deviation
The standard deviation is a widely measurement for the average amount by which data points deviate from the mean in any given dataset. It is given by the following equation: \[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}}\]
Immediately, you'll notice that parts of this equation is not so different from the one that gives us the Mean Absolute Deviation. The numerator for both equations gives the sum of the differences for each value from the mean, which is then divided by the total number of datapoints to find the average deviation. However, the standard deviation has this term squared, while the MAD just uses the raw values.

Observe:
MAD --> \(\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar x)}{N}\)
Standard Deviation --> \(\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}\)

Notice that all we've really done is squared the numerator of the Mean Absolute Deviation. Following this, we take the square root of the entire term in order to normalize the values in accordance with the original dataset. i.e. \[\sqrt{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}}\] The key intuition behind this, is that in squaring the sum of the differences from the mean, we give much more weight to values that deviate further from the mean than the others. When compared with the values produced by the Mean Absolute Deviation, you'll notice that the standard deviation actually produces a slight error because of this - hence the reason why it is also known as 'the standard error of the mean'.

Variance
Variance: Variance quantifies the average squared deviation of each data point from the mean. It takes into account the entire dataset and provides a measure of how much the values vary from the mean.
